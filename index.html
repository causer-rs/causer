Causally inspired Sequential Recommendation

Anonymous Author  

1. Abstract

The key of sequential recommendation lies in the accurate item correlation modeling. Previous models infer such information based on item co-occurrences, which may fail to capture the real causal relations, and impact the recommendation performance and robustness. In this paper, we equip sequential recommendation with a novel causal discovery module to capture causalities among items. In general, we firstly assume that item correlations are determined by an underlying causal graph. Then we jointly learn the causal graph and sequential recommender model by fitting the observed user behaviors. More specifically, in order to satisfy causality requirements, the causal graph is regularized by a differentiable directed acyclic constraint. Considering that the number of items in recommender systems can be very large, we represent each item as a mixture of latent clusters, and the causal graph is defined on the cluster level, which enhances the model scalability and robustness. In addition, we provide theoretical analysis on the identifiability of our causal graph in the context of sequential modeling. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that item causal relations are considered in the field of sequential recommendation. In the experiments, we apply our framework to different sequential recommender models, and extensive empirical studies demonstrate that our framework is effective in promoting the recommendation performance and explainability.

Contributions

  • we propose to enhance sequential recommendation by capturing causal relations among user behaviors, which, to the best of our knowledge, is the first time in the recommendation domain.
  • To realize the above idea, we design a general framework by infusing a causal discovery module into sequential recommender models. To make this framework more scalable, we further propose an encoder-decoder architecture to define the causal graph on the cluster-level.
  • We provide theoretical analysis on the identifiability of our causal graph in the context of sequential models.
  • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework in promoting the recommendation performance and explainability.
